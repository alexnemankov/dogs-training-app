### Part 2: Lecture "From a Harsh Predator to a Couch Potato"

**1. The Great Divide: We Didn’t Catch Them!**

For a long time, it was believed that a rugged caveman stole a wolf cub, raised it, and—voila!—man's best friend was born. But modern scientists (and common sense) say, "Unlikely." Wolves are dangerous predators, and keeping them in a cave with infants is a terrible business strategy.

It was most likely **self-domestication**. The friendliest and least fearful wolves started hanging around human camps to scavenge leftovers. Those who didn't growl at humans got more calories, survived, and produced similarly "friendly" offspring.

> **Funny Visualization:** A cartoon where a grim wolf looks at a human campfire and says: _"I could eat them... but they have roasted bones and I think they're about to invent ear scratches. I’m in."_

**2. Belyaev’s Experiment: Why Do They Have Spots?**

Why do domestic animals look different? Floppy ears, curly tails, white spots on the forehead... It turns out all of this comes as a "package deal" with friendliness.

Soviet geneticist Dmitry Belyaev proved this with foxes. By selecting only the tamest individuals, within a few generations, he got foxes that not only wagged their tails but started looking like dogs!

**The Essence:** Stress hormones (adrenaline) and joy hormones (serotonin) are linked to pigmentation and skeletal development. By choosing "kind," we accidentally chose "spotted and floppy-eared."

**3. Neoteny: Eternal Children**

A dog is essentially a wolf that got stuck in childhood.

- Wolves have a strict **socialization window** (a couple of weeks), after which anything unfamiliar is an enemy.
    
- In dogs, this window is wider, and they retain curiosity and playfulness into old age.
    

This is also morphologically noticeable: dogs have shorter muzzles and larger eyes relative to their skulls. This triggers the **"baby schema"** in us and a biological urge to feed them.

**4. Dogs Learned to Look Us in the Eye**

This is a unique trick. Wolves perceive a direct stare as a challenge or a threat. Dogs, however, use eye contact to:

- Ask for help.
    
- Trigger an **oxytocin** (the love hormone) release in us.
    

When you look at a dog and they look back, oxytocin levels in both of you soar—just like between a mother and an infant. We have literally "hacked" each other's biological systems.

**5. Key Differences: Dog vs. Wolf**

|**Feature**|**Wolf**|**Dog**|
|---|---|---|
|**Brain**|Larger (needs to survive in the wild)|20-30% smaller (we took over some functions)|
|**Digestion**|Pure carnivore|Ability to digest starch (thanks to life by the campfire)|
|**Communication**|Complex within the pack|Directed toward humans|
