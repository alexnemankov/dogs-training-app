### Lecture 1: The Philosophy of Behaviorism. How to "Read" a Dog Through Symptoms

**Introduction: The Dog as a Closed Book** Imagine you’ve arrived in a country where you don't speak the language. You see a local waving their arms and shouting loudly. Is he angry, asking for help, or warning of an approaching storm? Most dog owners are in this exact situation. When a dog barks, they see "bad behavior." A behaviorist, however, sees a message. Our goal in this course is to learn that language.

**Part 1: The "Behavior Iceberg" Concept** The first thing to learn: Behavior is only the tip of the iceberg.

- **Above the water (what we see):** Barking, jumping, aggression, digging, refusing to walk on a leash. These are **symptoms**.
    
- **Under the water (the causes):**
    
    1. **Physiology:** Pain, hunger, hormones, lack of sleep.
        
    2. **Emotions:** Fear, frustration, joy, anxiety.
        
    3. **Environment:** Too noisy, too cramped, lack of a sense of security.
        
    4. **Genetics:** Breed instincts (e.g., herding instinct).
        

**Key Takeaway:** It’s useless to try and "cut off" the tip of the iceberg (banning the bark) if we don't address what’s underwater. If a dog barks out of fear and we punish them for it, the fear doesn’t go away—it just finds another exit (for example, through a bite).

**Part 2: Observation vs. Interpretation** This is the hardest skill for a human. We are social beings prone to **anthropomorphism** (attributing human qualities).

- **Interpretation (Error):** "He’s doing it to spite me," "He knows he’s guilty," "He’s dominating my wife."
    
- **Observation (Fact):** "The dog pinned its ears back, averted its gaze, its body is tense, it is trembling slightly."
    

**The "Video Camera" Rule:** If what you are describing cannot be filmed and measured (e.g., you can't measure "resentment," but you can measure the "frequency of lip licking"), then it is your imagination. To help a dog, we must operate only with facts.

**Part 3: The S-O-R-C Formula (Breaking Behavior into Atoms)** To understand any action, we use a scientific analysis model:

- **S (Stimulus/Signal):** What happened a second before the action? (Doorbell rang, a cat ran by).
    
- **O (Organism):** Who is this dog? (Did she sleep well today? Is she young or old? Do her joints ache? Is she full?).
    
- **R (Reaction):** What exactly did she do? (Jumped at the door, growled).
    
- **C (Consequence):** How did it end? (Owner shouted = attention received; Cat ran away = victory).
    

If the consequence (C) is beneficial to the dog, they will repeat the behavior again and again.

**Part 4: Ethics: The LIMA Principle and the Five Freedoms** We work according to the **LIMA** system (Least Intrusive, Minimally Aversive)—choosing the gentlest and most effective path. Before teaching a dog anything new, we must check if their **Five Freedoms** are met:

1. Freedom from thirst and hunger.
    
2. Freedom from discomfort (a comfy place to sleep).
    
3. Freedom from pain and disease.
    
4. Freedom to express natural behavior (digging, sniffing, socializing).
    
5. Freedom from fear and distress.
    

If even one point is violated, the behavior will "break," and no amount of training will help.

1. Break it down using the **S-O-R-C** formula.
    
2. Try to describe this behavior for 2 minutes using only verbs and facts, as if you were an impartial video camera.
